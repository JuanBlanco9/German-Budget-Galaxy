Public Interest Data Project

What this dataset is

A compiled dataset from 13 official public primary sources documenting German public funding flows to NGOs. Each record has a confidence_level (verified/estimated/inferred) and a traceable source_url. This dataset documents funding flows — it does not attribute conduct or intent to any organization.

Acceptable use

  • Journalism, academic research, public policy analysis
  • Personal, educational, and non-profit use
  • Reproduction with attribution to primary sources

Prohibited use

  • Attributing criminal, fraudulent, or immoral conduct to any organization based solely on this dataset
  • Presenting data as “exclusive” when the primary source is publicly available
  • Combining with personal data without appropriate legal basis

Key limitations

  • 92% of OECD CRS records use generic channel names — no individual organization names. This is a documented finding about Germany's reporting architecture, not a data error.
  • Political foundations (~€700M/year): federal funding known, redistribution to local partners is opaque
  • Churches (Caritas/Diakonie): ~€1.5B/year redistribution is private, zero coverage
  • 13 of 16 German states have no public subsidy database

About Budget Galaxy

A reference tool for public spending across four countries. Updated April 2026.
Methodology

Budget Galaxy aggregates public spending data from official sources in four countries. The structure below summarizes the pipeline for each country.

United Kingdom
  • Central government: HM Treasury OSCAR II, covering approximately 55 departments, executive agencies and bodies. The 2023 OSCAR edition was reprocessed to correct a £114B NHS artifact that would otherwise double-count health spending.
  • NHS: trust-level operating cost breakdowns for 206 provider trusts (118 acute, 45 mental health, 18 community, 15 specialist, 10 ambulance), with workforce and cost-type metadata where published. NHS commissioner (ICB) and provider (trust) values are netted against each other to avoid double-counting.
  • Local government: 401 English local authorities from MHCLG Revenue Outturn, plus 19 councils with supplier-level spend parsed from their own published registers and classified into MHCLG service categories. An additional 7 police and fire authorities are covered as separate entities.
  • Population data: ONS mid-2023 local authority estimates (residents, all ages, both sexes), attached to the 19 councils for per-capita analysis. Police and fire authorities do not receive population since they are service areas, not geographic entities.
Germany, France, United States
  • Federal budgets from the equivalent official sources (Bundeshaushalt, Loi de Finances / Budget de l’État, OMB Budget of the United States Government).
  • Multi-level consolidation (federal to state to local) is documented per country where the accounting crosses levels.

The full data pipeline is in the project repository. Every number on this site can be traced back to a specific line in a specific source file.

What went wrong

Public spending data has gaps, inconsistencies, and quirks that most transparency tools hide. Budget Galaxy documents them here because they matter for how you read the numbers.

Birmingham City Council is absent from the 2023-24 tree. Birmingham issued a Section 114 notice in September 2023, which disrupted their MHCLG submission pipeline. Their public spend register (City Observatory) uses a rolling 24-month window that has since purged 2023-24 transactions. We tried six alternative sources and recovered nothing. The 2024-25 data exists but cannot be injected because Birmingham has no node in the MHCLG-derived tree for that year.
Cornwall’s supplier totals are under review. Cornwall’s MHCLG net current expenditure (£1.0B) is consistent with other unitary authorities. But the supplier-level spend derived from Cornwall’s raw CSV sums to approximately £6.2B — roughly 6x the MHCLG operating value, compared to a ratio closer to 1.1x for other councils. The most likely explanation is that Cornwall’s “Net Amount” column includes capital expenditure and internal transfers alongside operating spend. Until this is reconciled, treat Cornwall’s supplier figures as an upper bound.
NHS commissioner and provider spending overlap. NHS Integrated Care Boards commission services from NHS Trusts. Summing ICB commissioning and Trust operating costs directly would double-count the same money. Budget Galaxy nets these values against each other. If you compare to a source that doesn’t do this netting, expect your totals to be larger than ours.
OSCAR 2023 had a £114B health artifact. The 2023 edition of HM Treasury’s OSCAR II dataset contained a £114B discrepancy in health spending that appears to be an accounting-transition artifact from the shift to OSCAR II. We removed it. Our 2023 UK total is therefore lower than a naive OSCAR 2023 sum by approximately that amount. The same "n/a" NHS Trusts sub-bucket pattern exists in the 2020, 2021, and 2022 trees and was removed in the same way.
Local government depth is England-only. Each of the four UK nations has its total public spending captured at the devolved administration level (Scottish Government £36.84B, Welsh Assembly Government £23.90B, Northern Ireland Executive £33.21B), but only England has a council-by-council breakdown inside Local Government (England). The reason is regulatory: England’s 401 local authorities all report to MHCLG in a standardized Revenue Outturn format. Scotland’s 32 councils report to the Accounts Commission, Wales’s 22 to Welsh Government, and Northern Ireland’s 11 districts to DoF NI — three separate data regimes with different schemas. Integrating them would require three independent pipelines carefully netted against the devolved government totals already captured, to avoid double-counting. For now, treat Scotland, Wales, and NI local spending as aggregated inside their devolved administration node, not broken down by council. The 19 councils with supplier-level detail are also all English for the same reason.

Germany, France, and the United States have their own data quirks documented in the source repository. This page focuses on the UK because it is where we have the deepest coverage.

Who built this and why

Budget Galaxy is built and maintained by an independent developer. The project is open and every number is traceable to an official source.

The project exists because public spending data in most countries is technically published but practically unreadable. Central government datasets are scattered across official portals, local government data is fragmented across hundreds of councils, and cross-country comparison is almost impossible without manual reconciliation. Budget Galaxy is one attempt at closing that gap for four countries.

The data, the pipeline, and the full source are in the project repository.

Über German Budget Galaxy

Was ist das?

Eine interaktive Visualisierung der gesamten öffentlichen Ausgaben in Deutschland, den USA, Frankreich und Großbritannien. Allein für Deutschland: €2,3 Billionen — aufgeschlüsselt nach Bundeshaushalt, Sozialversicherungen (GKV, Rente, Arbeitslosenversicherung, Pflege, Unfallversicherung), 16 Bundesländern und Kommunen.

Jede Kugel, die Sie sehen, stellt eine reale Haushaltsposition dar. Die Größe ist proportional zum Betrag. Klicken Sie auf eine Kugel, um zu sehen, was sich darin befindet. Zoomen Sie hinein für Details. Jeder Euro, Dollar und jedes Pfund ist einer offiziellen Quelle zuordenbar.

Warum gibt es das?

Der Bundeshaushalt ist öffentlich. Er wird jährlich vom Bundesministerium der Finanzen veröffentlicht. Jeder kann ihn einsehen.

Aber „öffentlich“ ist nicht dasselbe wie „zugänglich“. Die offiziellen Daten verteilen sich auf tausende Seiten von Einzelplänen in einem Format, das für parlamentarische Verfahren konzipiert ist, nicht für öffentliches Verständnis. Zu wissen, dass €44,8 Mrd. in die Rentenversicherung fließen, ist nicht dasselbe wie es neben den €47 Mio. für den Bundespräsidenten zu sehen.

Dieses Projekt macht die Struktur sichtbar. Nicht um für mehr oder weniger Ausgaben in irgendeinem Bereich zu argumentieren, sondern um jedem — Bürger, Journalist, Student, Forscher — ein unmittelbares, intuitives Verständnis davon zu geben, wohin das Geld fließt.

Drei Werkzeuge

Budget Galaxy

Der visuelle Einstiegspunkt. Eine zoombare Kreispackungsvisualisierung, in der jedes Ministerium eine Kugel ist und innerhalb jedes Ministeriums Kapitel und Titel verschachtelt sind. Die größte Kugel — Arbeit und Soziales mit €175,7 Mrd. — ist unmöglich zu übersehen. Das ist der Punkt.

Budget Explorer

Das analytische Werkzeug. Navigieren Sie die Haushaltshierarchie mit einem Brotkrumenpfad. Jede Ebene zeigt eine Aufschlüsselung mit Prozenten, Ausgabenartanalyse und angereichertem Kontext — wer profitiert, wie viel pro Person, wie Deutschland international vergleicht.

Budget Evolution

Die historische Ansicht. Zehn Jahre Haushaltsdaten (2015–2024), die zeigen, wie sich die Ausgaben verändert haben. Der COVID-Anstieg. Die Zeitenwende in der Verteidigung. Die Explosion der Zinskosten.

Datenquellen

Deutschland: Bundeshaushalt über bundeshaushalt.de, Sozialversicherungsdaten von GKV-Spitzenverband, DRV Bund, Bundesagentur für Arbeit; Länderhaushalte und Kommunalfinanzen über Destatis. USA: USAspending.gov. Frankreich: data.gouv.fr (PLF). UK: HM Treasury OSCAR.

Anreicherungsdaten (Empfängerzahlen, internationale Vergleiche) stammen von offiziellen Statistiken der jeweiligen Behörden: Deutsche Rentenversicherung, Bundesagentur für Arbeit, OECD, NATO, Destatis und andere.

Einschränkungen

• Der Bundeshaushalt zeigt den Soll-Haushalt, nicht die tatsächlichen Ausgaben (Ist).

• Außeretatmäßige Posten wie das Sondervermögen Bundeswehr (€100 Mrd.) und der Klima- und Transformationsfonds sind nicht enthalten.

• Die Gesamtdarstellung (€2,3 Bio.) enthält Überschneidungen zwischen Ebenen (z.B. Bundeszuschüsse zur Rentenversicherung). Der konsolidierte Gesamtbetrag ist niedriger.

• Anreicherungsdaten sind näherungsweise und aus verschiedenen Berichtszeiträumen (meist 2023–2024).

Jeder Euro, der von Bürgerinnen und Bürgern erhoben wird, sollte verständlich sein für diese Bürger. Nicht in parlamentarischen Formaten eingeschlossen. Nicht hinter Fachjargon versteckt. Sichtbar, erkundbar und vergleichbar — das ist, was dieses Werkzeug bietet.

What this dataset is

This is a compiled dataset from 13 official public primary sources documenting German public funding flows to non-governmental organizations. It covers the period 2010–2025 and contains 528,000+ records.

Every record carries a confidence_level field (verified, estimated, or inferred) and a traceable source_url linking to the original primary source. The dataset documents funding flows — it does not attribute conduct, intent, or wrongdoing to any organization.

Statistical patterns in the dataset are detected by automated queries. They indicate data points that warrant further investigation by qualified journalists or researchers. They are not accusations.
